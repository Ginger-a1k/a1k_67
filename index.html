New Media Challenges, Text Legibility & CTML Principles

PART B - Q1: New Media Challenges (10 marks)

1. Information Overload

Excessive amount of content makes it difficult for users to find relevant information.

Example: Social media feeds with endless scrolling (Facebook, Twitter) where users struggle to filter important content from noise.
2. Attention Span Reduction

Digital media consumption has shortened user attention spans, requiring quick, engaging content.

Example: TikTok and Instagram Reels succeed with 15-60 second videos, while longer content struggles to retain viewers.
3. Platform Fragmentation

Content must be adapted for multiple platforms with different formats and requirements.

Example: A video needs vertical format for TikTok (9:16), square for Instagram (1:1), and horizontal for YouTube (16:9).
4. Rapid Technological Change

New tools, formats, and platforms emerge constantly, requiring continuous learning.

Example: The shift from SD to HD to 4K to 8K video; emergence of VR/AR content and AI-powered editing tools.
5. Credibility and Misinformation

Easy content creation and sharing has led to spread of fake news and manipulated media.

Example: Deepfake videos and AI-generated content that can convincingly mimic real people and events.
6. Monetization Pressure

Content creators face challenges in generating sustainable income from their work.

Example: YouTube's changing ad revenue policies and demonetization affecting content creators' earnings.
7. Copyright and Intellectual Property

Digital media makes it easy to copy and redistribute content without permission.

Example: Music and video content being re-uploaded without licenses, leading to copyright strikes and legal issues.
8. Privacy and Data Security

New media platforms collect vast amounts of user data, raising privacy concerns.

Example: Social media apps tracking user behavior, location, and personal information for targeted advertising.
9. Digital Divide and Accessibility

Not everyone has equal access to technology and high-speed internet.

Example: Rural areas with limited broadband access struggle to stream high-quality video content or participate in online learning.
10. Algorithm Dependency

Content visibility depends heavily on platform algorithms, limiting organic reach.

Example: Instagram and Facebook algorithms prioritize engagement over chronological order, making it harder for new creators to gain visibility.

Q2: 4 Factors of Text Legibility

1. Font Choice and Typography

The typeface selection significantly impacts readability.

  • Sans-serif fonts (Arial, Helvetica) are better for screens
  • Serif fonts (Times New Roman) work well for print
  • Avoid overly decorative or script fonts for body text
  • Maintain consistent font family throughout
2. Size and Spacing

Proper sizing and spacing ensure comfortable reading.

  • Font size: Minimum 16px for body text on screens
  • Line height: 1.5 to 1.6 times the font size
  • Letter spacing: Adequate space between characters
  • Paragraph spacing: Clear separation between blocks
3. Contrast and Color

Sufficient contrast between text and background is essential.

  • High contrast: Dark text on light background (or vice versa)
  • WCAG standards: Minimum 4.5:1 ratio for normal text
  • Avoid bright colors that strain eyes
  • Consider colorblind users (don't rely on color alone)
4. Text Length and Alignment

Line length and alignment affect reading flow.

  • Line length: 50-75 characters per line for optimal reading
  • Alignment: Left-aligned for Western languages (easier to read)
  • Avoid justified text on screens (creates uneven spacing)
  • Break long content into shorter paragraphs

Q3: 3 Benefits of Video Editing Software

1. Creative Control and Flexibility

Video editing software provides extensive tools for creative expression.

  • Multiple editing options: Cut, trim, split, merge clips easily
  • Effects and transitions: Add professional-looking effects
  • Color grading: Adjust colors, brightness, and contrast
  • Audio control: Mix, balance, and enhance sound
  • Non-destructive editing: Make changes without altering original files
2. Time and Cost Efficiency

Modern software streamlines the editing process and reduces costs.

  • Faster workflow: Automated tools and shortcuts save time
  • Templates and presets: Quick application of common styles
  • Cost-effective: Eliminates need for expensive hardware studios
  • Batch processing: Apply effects to multiple clips simultaneously
  • Instant preview: See changes in real-time before rendering
3. Professional Quality Output

Achieve broadcast-quality results with advanced features.

  • High-resolution support: Edit 4K, 8K, and HDR content
  • Multi-format export: Output for different platforms and devices
  • Advanced features: Motion tracking, chroma keying, stabilization
  • Integration: Works with other creative software (After Effects, Photoshop)
  • Collaboration tools: Share projects and work with teams remotely

PART C - Principles of CTML (Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning) (20 marks)

1. Multimedia Principle

People learn better from words and pictures together than from words alone.

  • Combine narration with relevant visuals
  • Use diagrams, animations, and images to support text
  • Dual-channel processing enhances understanding
Example: A science lesson showing both animated diagrams of the water cycle AND verbal explanation is more effective than text alone.
2. Contiguity Principle (Spatial & Temporal)

Related words and pictures should be presented near each other in space and time.

  • Spatial: Place text labels close to corresponding graphics
  • Temporal: Present narration and animation simultaneously
  • Reduces cognitive load by eliminating search time
Example: In a tutorial video, show the action on screen while describing it, rather than explaining first then showing later.
3. Modality Principle

Use spoken narration rather than on-screen text when presenting graphics or animations.

  • Audio narration + visuals work better than text + visuals
  • Prevents overload of visual channel
  • Allows learners to process information through both channels
Example: An instructional video uses voiceover to explain a process while showing visual demonstration, instead of displaying text paragraphs on screen.
4. Redundancy Principle

Don't present the same information in multiple formats simultaneously if it creates overload.

  • Avoid presenting identical spoken narration and on-screen text together
  • Choose either audio OR text, not both with same content
  • Exception: Captions are acceptable for accessibility
Example: Don't show text on screen that exactly matches the voiceover word-for-word; this creates cognitive overload.
5. Coherence Principle

Exclude extraneous material that doesn't support the learning objective.

  • Remove decorative images and unnecessary sounds
  • Avoid interesting but irrelevant information
  • Keep content focused and concise
Example: Remove background music, decorative animations, or fun facts that distract from the core lesson content.
6. Personalization Principle

Use conversational style and direct address to engage learners.

  • Use "you" and "your" instead of "one" or "the learner"
  • Adopt friendly, conversational tone
  • Creates social presence and increases engagement
Example: Say "You will learn how to edit videos" instead of "The learner will learn how to edit videos."
7. Segmenting Principle

Break complex content into smaller, manageable segments.

  • Divide lessons into bite-sized chunks
  • Allow learner control over pacing
  • Provide clear breaks between segments
Example: A 30-minute tutorial is broken into six 5-minute chapters that users can complete at their own pace.
8. Pre-training Principle

Introduce key concepts and terminology before presenting complex material.

  • Provide overview of main components first
  • Define important terms upfront
  • Build foundation before adding complexity
Example: Before teaching video editing techniques, first explain basic terms like "timeline," "clip," "transition," and "render."
9. Signaling Principle

Highlight essential information to guide attention.

  • Use highlighting, arrows, or animations to emphasize key points
  • Provide clear headings and organizational cues
  • Direct attention to important elements
Example: Use a red circle or arrow to point out specific buttons or features in a software tutorial video.
10. Voice Principle

Use human voice rather than machine-generated voice for narration.

  • Human voice creates social presence
  • Increases learner engagement and motivation
  • More natural and easier to understand
Example: Record narration with a real person rather than using text-to-speech or robotic AI voice.
